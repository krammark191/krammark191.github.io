The First Man on the Moon

In 1961 President John F. Kennedy announced he wanted to put men on the moon. NASA worked hard to get people into space, and they were determined to put people on the moon. Not only did they need a space craft capable of getting them to the moon, but they also needed to get the astronauts back off the moon and land safely on Earth again. Apollo 11 blasted off on July 16, 1969. Neil Armstrong, Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin and Michael Collins were the astronauts on Apollo 11. Four days after launch, the Apollo 11 crew landed on the moon in the lunar lander dubbed "The Eagle." Astronaut Michael Collins stayed behind in orbit around the moon to do experiments and take pictures while Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin made history by being the first human beings to ever step foot on not only the moon, but a naturally formed object in space other than Earth. The two astronauts spent about three hours on the moon and left behind a United States of America flag as well as a sign. In less than ten years, President Kennedy's dream came true. The world made a monumental leap in space exploration.

The International Space Station

The International Space Station was launched into low-earth orbit on November 23, 1998. The ISS was originally only planned to be used for a limited amount of time, but the lifespan of the station has been extended multiple times, and currently stands at 2028.
